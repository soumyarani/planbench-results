Subgoal Interactions in Planning — A Complete Taxonomy

Navigate: Planning Fundamentals PlanBench Results Transitive Dependencies Cross-Benchmark

When a planning problem has multiple goals, those goals can interact. Understanding these interactions is central to planning — and to evaluating whether LLM benchmarks actually test planning reasoning. This page defines the complete taxonomy, grounded in the planning literature (Korf 1987, Barrett & Weld 1993, Hoffmann et al. 2004).

Three Dimensions of Subgoal Interaction

Subgoal interactions have three independent dimensions. Asking “is this interaction bipartite or destructive?” is like asking “is this fruit red or sweet?” — those are different properties of the same thing.

1. Structural Cause

WHY does the interaction exist?

  • Shared goal variables
  • Init-state coupling
  • Transitive chain
  • No coupling

2. Behavioral Type

WHAT happens at execution?

  • Independent
  • Synergistic (helps)
  • Destructive (harms)
  • Non-serializable

3. Graph Topology

SHAPE of the full interaction graph

  • Chain
  • Clique
  • Star
  • Bipartite
  • Disconnected
These dimensions are orthogonal. A bipartite topology can produce synergistic or destructive behavior. A destructive interaction can arise from shared variables or init-state coupling. The graph topology is a property of the full goal set, not of individual pairs.

Instance-20: on(B,A) and on(C,D) — goals share NO blocks ({B,A} ∩ {C,D} = ∅). But in the init state, C sits on A and B sits on D — each goal’s blocks are tangled with the other’s.

Transitive Chain

Two goals share no blocks directly, but are connected through a chain of intermediate goals that do share blocks.

Instance-458: on(A,C) and on(B,E) share no blocks ({A,C} ∩ {B,E} = ∅). But on(C,B) shares C with the first and B with the second, creating a chain: on(B,E) → on(C,B) → on(A,C).

No Coupling

Goals involve disjoint blocks AND the init state has no cross-stacking. Truly independent — can be achieved in any order at no extra cost.

Dimension 2: Behavioral Type

What happens when you try to achieve these goals? Determined by simulation or formal landmark analysis.

TypeDefinitionHow to DetectBlocksworld Example
Independent Achieving G1 has no effect on the cost of G2 cost(G2 after G1) = cost(G2 from init) on(A,B) and on(C,D) with flat init, no cross-stacking
Synergistic Achieving G1 makes G2 cheaper or free cost(G2 after G1) < cost(G2 from init) on(A,B) and ontable(C) when init has C on A — unstacking C helps both
Destructive (asymmetric) One ordering harms, the other doesn’t cost(G2 after G1) > cost(G2), but cost(G1 after G2) ≤ cost(G1) on(A,B) and on(B,C) — the Sussman anomaly
Destructive (symmetric) Both orderings make the other harder Both directions show cost increase Rare in Blocksworld
Non-serializable No total ordering achieves both goals without interleaving Every ordering requires undo/redo Classic Sussman anomaly with specific init states

The Sussman Anomaly = Asymmetric Destruction

For on(A,B) and on(B,C): achieving on(A,B) first puts A on B, making B not clear — so on(B,C) costs +2 extra steps (undo and redo). But achieving on(B,C) first leaves A unaffected, so on(A,B) costs the same. One direction harms, the other doesn’t — this asymmetry is what makes ordering critical.

Detection: Fast Downward Landmark Analysis

Fast Downward’s landmark analysis (Hoffmann et al. 2004, Richter et al. 2008) is the established method that captures interactions from all structural causes. It works by:

Step 1: Build a relaxed planning graph from the initial state — this is where init-state dependencies enter the analysis
Step 2: Backchain from each goal fact, finding landmarks — facts that must become true at some point in every valid plan
Step 3: Compute orderings between landmarks:
• Greedy-necessary: A must hold immediately before B is first achieved — a direct, strict ordering
• Natural: A must be true at some point before B — can be transitive through intermediate landmarks
Structural CauseHow Fast Downward Detects It
Shared goal variablesGoals share achiever preconditions directly
Init-state couplingBackchaining discovers init-state facts as landmarks (e.g., clear(A) requires moving C that sits on A)
Transitive chainTransitive closure of landmark orderings connects non-adjacent goals
